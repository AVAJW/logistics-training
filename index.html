The Basics

A freight broker is the middleman between a shipper (the person or company that needs goods moved) and a carrier (a trucking company or driver that hauls the freight).

  • You don‚Äôt own trucks.
  • You coordinate the movement of freight.
  • You get paid by the shipper, and you pay the carrier‚Äîyou keep the difference (your margin).

Broker‚Äôs Core Role:

  • Find capacity (trucks) for shippers.
  • Negotiate rates on both ends.
  • Track shipments from pickup to delivery.
  • Solve issues fast: delays, breakdowns, bad weather, etc.

Module 4: Getting a Rate

Getting the right rate is a key skill for freight brokers. Here‚Äôs what you need to know:

Get the Load Details:

  • Equipment Type
  • Zip Codes / City and State
  • Weight and Commodity
  • Target Rate

If the Sales Rep can get more information:

  • Pick Up and Delivery Windows (Can it pick up all week? All month?)
  • Can it go on a Reefer? On a Step Deck? On a HotShot?
  • Could anything radically alter the rate (Hazmat, Over-Dimensional, Job-Site Delivery)

The time needed to get a quote back to a Customer will vary by account. Some Customers are looking for the cheapest rate and will wait for hours for someone to find an option they like. Others will take the first option. If you are unfamiliar with an account, always ask the Sales Rep how quickly they need pricing back. If they do not have a preference, default to the 30 minute rule.

  • Look at the lane on Google Maps - note mileage, proximity to big cities, ports, etc.
  • Look for lane history in LAVA and call a few past carriers to see if they are available.
  • Look at available trucks on the boards - DAT / ITS - and make outbound calls / emails.
  • Get a sense of the rate you want to pay and get on the phone.
  • Start low! (Ex. If the target rate in the load says $1000, start at $800 - you may get lucky)
  • End with an odd number. (Ex. ‚ÄúI only have $936 in this load‚Äù) it sometimes works in unexpected ways.
  • Say a rate, then stop talking. Let the carrier respond. This can force the carrier to negotiate with themselves and get you a better price.
  • Be confident. ‚ÄúThe load IS paying X‚Äù not ‚ÄúI am trying to be around X‚Äù. Negotiate like you are willing to walk away, even if you are not (don‚Äôt lose the carrier if you really need them). If they know you need them you are not negotiating from a position of strength, thus losing money right off the bat.
  • Know the load details (this will help with confidence). Sell the best load details (Ex. It‚Äôs light. The Receiver‚Äôs open 24/7. No driver touch. We have a reload coming out of that area.)
  • Learn as you go. Identify objections (weather, location, etc) and address them on the next call before the carrier brings it up. (Ex. Hello, I have a load from Reno to Sac. It‚Äôs snowing over the pass a bit, so I have a little extra on the rate to help your driver out. We‚Äôre at $800.) This will put you in a position of strength from the start.
  • Don‚Äôt immediately settle when you hear your price on a call in!!! (Ex. If you want to pay $1000 on a load and a carrier calls in and says ‚ÄúI‚Äôll run this for a thousand‚Äù, say ‚ÄúWe‚Äôre at $800.‚Äù Don‚Äôt lose the truck over this, but always take a shot at a better price).

Module 5: Quotes

Generating accurate and competitive quotes is fundamental to securing business. Here's how we approach it, especially when utilizing load boards:

Using the Load Boards - DAT (Dial-A-Truck) / ITS (Internet Truckstop):

  • Utilize the search feature. Email good matches and call Carriers with relevant area codes (home base). On DAT leave notes on the trucks you have called so we don‚Äôt do double the work.
  • Post on each load board (we will go over examples)
  • Don‚Äôt post the exact lane (Ex. If we have Moundhouse, NV to Madera, CA we would want to post Reno, NV to Fresno, CA) This will bring in more calls because people know the towns of Reno and Fresno, while they might not know places like Moundhouse, NV or Madera, CA. This also helps protect our customers in smaller towns.
  • Identify market trends as you‚Äôre using the boards. You will be able to get a sense of capacity (no calls = few carriers / phone is blowing up = many carriers). Communicate market dynamics to your salesperson.
  • Hot Prospects. This is a feature on Truckstop that you can use if you are striking out. It will give you a lot of options of people who have done this lane or have posted on this lane. It's also a great tool to build long term capacity in areas.
  • Different ways of posting can increase call in‚Äôs. If you are struggling, post from places that might be 150 miles away from the actual pickup. Also post it to a more desirable location. Even if they don‚Äôt want it you now have the ability to talk them into it.
  • Expanding filters. When searching for trucks don‚Äôt limit yourself to 100-150 miles. We have deadheaded trucks 1000+ miles before.

Module 6: Covering Pending Loads - You booked a Carrier

Once you've successfully booked a carrier for a load, there are crucial steps to ensure a smooth operation:

  • As you agree to work with a Carrier, check their MC# on Carrier411, RMIS/HWY, and in LAVA. If the Carrier is not set up, send them an email with a link to www.avacarriers.com. (SEE VETTING CARRIERS)
  • Confirm load details over the phone / email. Get contact information and the current location of the driver - city, state, and status. For same day / next day loads, check the feasibility of the driver‚Äôs pickup ETA (use Google Maps).
  • Review paperwork - ensure that everything is accurate, (i.e. rate, equipment type, PU and DL times). If there are documents specific to a location or customer that you need to send to the carrier, make sure they are included here.
  • Use LAVA to send the rate confirmation and tender. If there is something you want to draw attention to, you can add a note to the top of the email and highlight it.
  • Let Dispatch know you will be calling the Driver within the next few minutes.
  • Call the Driver to confirm the details of the shipment. Acquire his/her current location (city, state, and status). Review the specific expectations of the load with the driver.
  • If you have arranged MacroPoint usage, ask the driver if they will get on it at this time, and listen for their reaction.
  • Review Shippers and Receivers / confirm appointment times and shipping hours. Make sure the Salesperson has added contacts at shipper and receiver. Please check with your salesperson before calling shippers or receivers.

Module 7: Vetting Carriers

Thoroughly vetting carriers is paramount to ensure the safety and reliability of our operations. Here's a comprehensive guide:

DO NOT GET A LOAD STOLEN:

  • Check the Motor Carrier Number (MC) on Saferys/RMIS/Carrier411/LAVA. There are many companies with the same or similar company name - MC‚Äôs are very specific identifiers.
  • Look at Safer -You can click the FMCSA link in their LAVA cards and it will take you straight to the FMCSA website that has their official number listed. With theft running wild in the industry it will go a long way to protect AVA by either preventing us from using the wrong carrier, or by proving our due diligence if something nefarious does happen. If a carrier is not in LAVA and you want to verify their info, you can simply check the MC on Safer via this link. If their operating status says ‚ÄúApproved‚Äù then they are able to legally run freight for you. If it says ‚ÄúInactive‚Äù or ‚ÄúNot Authorized‚Äù you can UNDER NO CIRCUMSTANCE tender them a load.
  • Confirm that the phone number they are calling from matches their official number listed in Safer. If it doesn‚Äôt, YOU MUST call the number in Safer and verify the person you are speaking to is authorized to book freight for that company.
  • Just because a Carrier was good doesn't mean they are good. Do a fresh check (Ex. look at RMIS/HWY/Carrier411. Look at notes and colors in LAVA).
  • In LAVA if a carrier is denied, they will not be able to be put on the load. If they are conditional, look at the reason. If it is a service issue, check with your sales rep before putting them on a load. If you use them, YOU MUST put the driver on Macro Point.
  • ALWAYS LOOK CARRIERS UP BY MC OR DOT NUMBER. DO NOT LOOK THEM UP BY NAME.

On Carrier 411 check:

  • How many trucks and inspections they have. We need at least 1 inspection. If they only have 1 truck and you can hear 20 people talking in the background of the caller, this is a red flag.
  • How long their common or contract authority has been maintained. We need at least 6 months.
  • Does the carrier have broker authority? If so, ask if they will be using a company truck. You can require that they send you pictures of the truck they use to make sure they are not double brokering the load.
  • Does the company frequently change their business address? Has their address recently changed? These can be red flags.
  • Check their address on google maps. Is it something weird like a Taco Bell? Residential addresses aren‚Äôt uncommon.
  • Residential addresses aren't uncommon, but should be handled with extreme caution. A small carrier (1-2) trucks out a house makes sense. A large carrier out of a house makes no sense. Either way, you must check with management if a carrier is operating from a residential address.
  • Does the carrier have an online presence? A website and google reviews are good signs you are working with a legitimate carrier.
  • Look at delivery time vs miles and make sure it is feasible. (Ex. If we have a load picking up in Reno, NV Monday morning at 09:00 and delivering to El Paso, TX the following day at 14:00 we would need to book team drivers, as a solo driver would just not make it).
  • Use your gut instinct. If you have a feeling a driver is going to be shady and you aren‚Äôt confident they will do a good job on your load, don‚Äôt book them.
  • If they have freight guards, look at how recent the freight guard is. If the freightguard is less than a year or two old use caution. (The report will say the date and how many days old it is).
  • Look at how many there are. Is it just one freight guard 3 or more years ago or did it just happen?
  • Is there a response to it? In the freightguard it will explain the issue that caused the company to write the report. There will also be a section that gives the carrier a chance to tell their side of the story. A lot of freight guards are because of lazy brokering when these problems could have been caught sooner and been hashed out. Take the time to read the report!
  • How many power units does the company have? Look at this number if a company has 5 reports but 1,500 power units, it could be just one bad dispatcher
  • If you are unsure about using a carrier because of their freight guards, put them on macropoint. If they are not good with this request DO NOT USE THEM.
  • Ask open ended questions, do not ask questions that lead dispatchers to telling you what you want to hear. Ask the proper qualifying questions when booking trucks and identify answers to see if they are viable.
  • To perform a reverse image look up, go to Google Images, click the camera icon in the search bar, then either upload an image from your computer or paste the image URL to find where that image appears online; you can also right-click on an image and select "Search Google for Image" on most browsers to initiate a reverse image search directly from a webpage.
  • All loads must be on MacroPoint. MacroPoint can be used as a vetting tool. If DSP or DRV won‚Äôt use MacroPoint in the age of the smartphone it is usually a red flag, and if you are on the fence about using them it could be a good tool to help you decide. We don‚Äôt even need to put them on MacroPoint, we just want to hear the reaction.
  • Vetting doesn‚Äôt stop after you send a tender. YOU MUST speak to all drivers. The questions you ask when dispatching your driver will help weed out service failures and otherwise sketchy situations. See ‚ÄúPick Ups‚Äù below for the questions t be asked.

Module 8: Trafficking and Managing Freight (Freight Brokerage Support)

Effective management of active loads is crucial for timely deliveries and customer satisfaction.

Active Loads:

  • You must update all loads that have your name in them as the Broker. If you are working under a Sales Rep, you must update all of the loads in their name as well. (Filter by day of and previous day to make sure nothing is missed.) Every time you talk to or attempt to talk to a driver/disp, put notes in the load (‚Äúcalled, no answer so I sent email for update,etc..‚Äù)
  • If your load is marked ‚ÄúNeeds Attention‚Äù or ‚ÄúService Critical‚Äù make those loads the first call.
  • If the driver / dispatcher is unable to be reached and you have tried all methods of communication, escalate the issue up to the salesman.

Pre-Pick Up:

  • For next day pick ups, walk through basics in your mind and in LAVA.
  • Verify that all required carrier and driver information is in the load. If missing, check to see if it has been sent via email to the salesperson on the load, or call the dispatcher.
  • Verify shipper and receiver information is accurate. Ensure that there is a contact person at the shipper and receiver.
  • You can call the driver and introduce yourself. Ask them if it would be helpful for you to text them the pick up address. Ask them to text you when they are out for pick up.

Pick Ups:

  • Treat this call like you are screening the driver to make sure everything the dispatcher said when you booked the load was true.
  • Call the DRIVER for an update. Request the following information:
    • Confirm that they work for the carrier you booked with. (‚ÄúWhat company are you driving for?‚Äù) if they say they work for a different company, that means the load is double brokered.
    • Current location (city, state, and status) Check on google maps to see how far they are from the pickup. Don‚Äôt assume they‚Äôve checked.
    • ETA. Check if they have to stop and do anything before pickup (get gas, swap trailers, get food).
  • Review load details (Ex. Tarps, Scale Tickets, PPE, etc.). Make sure they know where the load is going. Don‚Äôt assume the dispatcher told them.
  • Confirm the PU address with them. Oftentimes a dispatcher will find a better paying load and pull their driver off. The driver will think you‚Äôre the broker on their new load and you won‚Äôt realize until you find out they‚Äôre heading to a different address.
  • Re-state the pick up window / appt time. Make sure they can make delivery at the specified time. (Do they have to stop and reset their hours?).
  • Tell the driver to call you and confirm when they make it to the shipper (NOTE: if they don't call you, you must reach out and confirm they made it).
  • Send a MacroPoint request before you make this call, and confirm that the driver is on it and will stay on for the duration of the load.
  • Ask them to get signed in and out times from the shipper.

In Transit:

  • Must check MacroPoint every day the load is in transit. If for some reason the driver isn‚Äôt on MacroPoint, or MacroPoint stops giving updates, call the Driver and make sure they‚Äôre good.
  • Check at the beginning and end of your shift.
  • Ask open-ended questions (‚ÄúWhere are you?‚Äù NOT ‚ÄúAre you in x location?‚Äù).
  • Add notes on current location (City, State, Status).
  • Double check ETD (estimated time of Delivery).
  • Ask them if they have a re-load already - see if they want one

Deliveries:

  • Call the DRIVER for an update (and add notes in LAVA).
  • (CITY, STATE, STATUS!!!)
  • ETA to scheduled delivery
  • If they are tracking late to the delivery, ask the following:
    • What is the nature of the delay?
    • What time will they arrive at the receiver
  • Verify that it looks like a legitimate time by searching their current location in Google Maps (traffic / weather / etc.)
  • Tell the driver to text you and confirm when they make it to the consignee / if they don't call, you must reach out and confirm they made it.
  • Ask them to get signed in and out times from the shipper.
  • If they have delivered, ask if there were any overages, shortages, or damages. If they say yes, find out the details
  • Get the POD (Proof of Delivery). Send it to AP@AVALOGISTICS.COM. Don‚Äôt put it in the load folder.
  • Check if the POD was signed. Was it signed by someone you‚Äôd expect to sign it, or by someone new?

Module 9: Building Relationships

Building strong relationships with carriers is key to long-term success and efficiency in freight brokering.

  • If you run a load with a carrier that did a good job for you, try to build a relationship with them and learn more about their company.
  • Ask about their number of trucks. Find out if the company has assets, or if they mostly have owner operators running under their MC. (This is important because then you can determine whether to build the relationship with the driver or with the dispatcher.)
  • Where does the Carrier like to run? Ask them questions such as do you prefer to stay as close to home as possible, do you like longhauls. Is there anywhere you won‚Äôt go? Do you have any dedicated lanes that you are always looking for backhauls on?
  • Do they currently run lanes that will regularly put their trucks in an area you have loads out of? This is very important, if you find this you will be able to dramatically increase your margin percentage. (Ex. Gabbs, NV to Neosho, MO)
  • Thank them for the service and give them the first opportunity at freight. This helps create the relationship better than anything. If you call your carrier before you go to the board it will be extremely beneficial for both parties. They will soon start calling you as well before they look on the boards knowing that they won‚Äôt get strung along by some other broker.
  • Call and build a carrier base in places that you regularly struggle out of/places you have a lot of freight out of. Try to be aware of places that you are getting a lot of quote requests from but not winning because of a lack of capacity. When you have a free moment in the day do things such as searching google for trucks (use unconventional methods) and finding some more carriers based in that area.
  • Ask when they will have a truck back in the area. Always do this! Even if you don‚Äôt have another load in that area. If they say yes, you can relay this information back to the salesman and he or she can go back to the customer and let them know that they will have another truck in the area and try to get another load.

Module 10: Additional Delivery Checks (Two-Factor Authentication)

To confirm a load has been delivered, we need at least one of the following in addition to a signed POD from the carrier:

  • Specific documents that can only be supplied by the receiver.
  • MacroPoint tracking the driver at the delivery location (Note how long they were there. Was it only 15 minutes, or a few hours?).
  • Confirmation from the receiver or customer (check with salesman if they want support doing this, or they will themselves).
